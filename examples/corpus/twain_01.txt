We are a crowd of steamship passengers, and we have been to Europe and the Holy Land. We have returned, our heads filled with pleasant memories of the journey, and our pockets filled with unpaid bills. We sailed from New York in the good ship Quaker City, on the fifth of June, 1867, and returned to the same port on the nineteenth of November following.

We visited Fayal, of the Azores; Gibraltar; Tangier, in Africa; Marseilles, Paris, and a part of France; Genoa, Leghorn, Civitavecchia, and Rome, in Italy; the Kingdom of Naples; Greece, Turkey, Syria, Lebanon, Palestine, Egypt, and Spain.

The Azores are a group of nine volcanic islands scattered about in the mid-Atlantic Ocean. They are a possession of Portugal, and they are so far from that country that they always seem to be in another world.

The islands are very fertile. One of them was covered, from the water's edge to the mountain summit, with a dense green forest. Another was nothing but sugar-cane, and another was all orange groves. The oranges were the finest I ever tasted—large, sweet, and lusciously juicy. I ate thirty or forty of them and developed no ill effects.

The people are very poor. They go barefoot, and work very hard, and yet they seem happy enough. The women carry everything on their heads. I saw them carrying a stone wall on their heads once. It was a stone wall of unusual size and solidity. I was impressed.
