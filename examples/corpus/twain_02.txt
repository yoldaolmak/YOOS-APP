Gibraltar is a rock. It is a very large rock. I know rocks, and I say with confidence that this is a very large one. It is nearly a mile in length, and a quarter of a mile wide, and looks upon the Strait of Gibraltar from a height of fourteen hundred feet. It is monstrous in size, and looks tremendously strong, and yet all the English fortifications are on top of it.

We were not allowed to carry a camera onto the rock. We were not allowed to sketch it. We were barely allowed to look at it. The English are very particular about Gibraltar. They have had it since 1704, and they intend to keep it.

The town of Gibraltar is a small place, built around the base of the rock. The streets are narrow and the buildings are old. The people are a mixture of English, Spanish, and Moors. They speak English, Spanish, and Arabic, sometimes all in the same sentence.

I found the view from the summit to be magnificent. On one side is the Mediterranean, shining like glass. On the other is the Atlantic. Africa is so close you could almost swim to it on a calm day. I say almost. I would not attempt it myself.

The fortifications are impressive. The English have tunneled through the solid rock, cut out chambers, mounted cannon, and made the place absolutely impregnable. Or so they say. I took their word for it.
